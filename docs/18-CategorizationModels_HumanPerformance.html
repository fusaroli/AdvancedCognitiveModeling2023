This chapter applies the same pipeline to real human data from Tylén et al. (2023). The structure has five parts: (1) Phase 0 conceptual interrogation specific to human data; (2) load, clean, and verify the data; (3) descriptive analysis of human learning curves and error patterns; (4) fit all three Stan models to individual participant data (Track 1); (5) NPE inference using the alien-task simulators from Chapter 15 (Track 2). 19.1 Phase 0: Structural Commitments for Human Data Analysis

Q1 — Is fitting the dangerous and nutritious decisions independently defensible with real human data?

Chapter 15’s Phase 0 Q2 established the independent-fits commitment in the context of forward simulation: the agent learns the two dimensions with separate parameters and separate sequential states. With human data, this commitment has empirical consequences that Chapter 15 could not address.

The commitment is defensible because: (a) feedback is provided for each dimension separately in the Tylén et al. (2023) task; (b) the two rules are logically independent (e.g., in Session 1, f1 AND f3 for dangerous and f4 for nutritious involve no shared features); (c) fitting independently is the simpler and more interpretable model.

The commitment could fail if: (a) humans jointly allocate attention across both decisions in a way that creates cross-decision correlations in response patterns; (b) the stimulus features that matter for one decision are also attended to for the other (e.g., if f4 is salient for nutritious, it may be sampled and influence the dangerous decision).

What we commit to here: independent fits, with the understanding that the GCM’s attention weight estimates may absorb any cross-dimension attention correlation as noise. A joint model could test this by checking whether attention weights estimated independently agree across the two decisions.

Q2 — What does “resetting model state across sessions” mean cognitively, and is it justified?

The Kalman filter’s prototype state is reset between sessions (new priors mu = 0.5, sigma = I). The GCM’s exemplar memory is also reset — the model starts Session 2 with no memory of Session 1 exemplars. This is a strong cognitive assumption: in reality, participants carry some representation from one session to the next (the stimuli are the same aliens; only the rules change).

We accept the reset assumption for two reasons: (a) the category rules change completely between sessions (the same alien that was dangerous in Session 1 may be peaceful in Session 2), so exemplar memories from Session 1 are actively misleading in Session 2; (b) the fitting is per-session, which means each session’s model is calibrated to that session’s rule structure independently, allowing us to compare model fits across sessions without confounding.

What we commit to here: per-session independent fits with state resets. The models do not carry memory across sessions. This is a design choice with cognitive content: we are testing whether each model can explain within-session learning, not cross-session transfer.

Q3 — Why use multilevel GCM but single-subject Kalman and rule models?

The multilevel GCM from Chapter 12 pools information across participants through a hierarchical prior on attention weights and sensitivity. The Kalman and rule models are fitted per-subject with no pooling. This creates a structural asymmetry:

  • For the GCM: partial pooling regularizes individual estimates, reducing overfitting and improving out-of-sample prediction especially for participants with few trials. The multilevel structure also produces a population-level summary of attention weights that is directly interpretable.
  • For the Kalman and rule models: per-subject fits are unregularized. For participants with short or homogeneous trial sequences, the posteriors may be broad and uninformative.

This asymmetry biases the LOO comparison in favor of the GCM by giving it an effective parameter reduction that the other models do not have. The honest interpretation is: the GCM with multilevel pooling competes against the Kalman and rule models without pooling. A fully fair comparison would require multilevel versions of all three models, which we do not develop here.

What we commit to here: we use the available multilevel GCM and per-subject Kalman/rule models, acknowledge the asymmetry explicitly in the model comparison section, and note that any ELPD advantage for the GCM should be interpreted with this caveat.

Q4 — What do we know about the AlienData.txt provenance and expected format?

The data file is from Tylén et al. (2023). Key known properties:

  • One row per trial; columns include subject, condition, session, cycle (block number), test (0 = training with feedback, 1 = test without feedback), stimulus (filename containing the 5-digit binary feature string), response (1–4, encoding all combinations of dangerous/nutritious), category (irrelevant here), dangerous and nutricious (true labels, note the spelling).
  • condition == 1 = individual participants; condition == 2 = pairs.
  • test == 0 trials are used for all model fitting (training trials only).
  • The nutricious spelling is present in the raw file; we rename it to nutritious in cleaning.
  • The rule verification block below is the halt-on-fail guard: if the assumed rules do not match the data labels, all downstream fitting is invalid and the chapter stops.

What we commit to here: we load the data, verify the stimulus parsing, verify the rule labelling, and halt if any verification fails. These checks mirror the Phase 0 Q3 verification in Chapter 15.

Summary of Phase 0 commitments. Independent fits per decision dimension and per session, with state resets between sessions. Multilevel GCM vs. per-subject Kalman/rule creates a pooling asymmetry that favors the GCM in LOO comparison. Prior hyperparameters match Chapters 14 and 16 (LOGIT_ERROR_PRIOR_MEAN = 0.0, SD = 1.5). Data verified against rules before any fitting proceeds.

19.5 4. Fitting Models to Human Data (Track 1)

We fit all three Stan models to the real human data. This is the fulfilment of the pipeline established in Chapters 12–16: prior predictive check → SBC → parameter recovery → single-agent fit → multilevel fit → LOO comparison, all now applied to empirical data.

19.5.1 Design Choices

Two-decision structure: We fit each model separately for each decision dimension within each session (Phase 0 Q1).

Sequential structure within sessions: Each session resets the model state (Phase 0 Q2).

Multilevel vs. single-subject: The multilevel GCM (Ch. 13, ch12_gcm_ml.stan) is used for all participants jointly. Kalman and rule models are fitted per-subject (Phase 0 Q3 asymmetry).

Note on the Stan rule model with 5 features: The Stan rule model in Chapter 14 hardcodes a candidate rule set over a 2D feature space. For the 5D Alien task, the candidate rule set must be expanded to include the session-specific relevant rules. A general approach enumerates all single-feature and two-feature threshold rules over the five binary dimensions: \(5 \times 2 + \binom{5}{2} \times 2 \times 2 = 10 + 40 = 50\) candidate rules. For binary features all thresholds are 0.5, so each rule reduces to testing whether one or two features equal 0 or 1. We leave this extension as an exercise; the rule model here uses the session-specific candidate rules derived from the rule-check above.

                           iter_w = 800, iter_s = 1000) {

    mod$pathfinder(data = data, seed = seed, num_paths = 4, refresh = 0),

n_features_alien <- 5L

# ── GCM simulator for alien task ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# theta = (logit_w1, ..., logit_w4, log_c) — 5 params (w5 = 1 - sum(w1:w4))
make_alien_gcm_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  function(theta) {
    # Softmax over 5 weights via log-sum-exp normalisation
    raw_w <- c(theta[1:4], 0)  # w5 is reference; use softmax
    w     <- exp(raw_w - log(sum(exp(raw_w))))
    c_val <- exp(theta[5])
    n <- nrow(obs_matrix)
    probs <- numeric(n)
    mem_obs <- matrix(NA_real_, 0, n_features_alien)
    mem_cat <- numeric(0)
    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      stim  <- obs_matrix[i, ]
      n_mem <- nrow(mem_obs)
      if (n_mem == 0 || length(unique(mem_cat)) < 2) {
        p <- 0.5
      } else {
        sims <- vapply(seq_len(n_mem), function(e)
          exp(-c_val * sum(w * abs(stim - mem_obs[e, ]))), numeric(1))
        s1 <- sum(sims[mem_cat == 1]); s0 <- sum(sims[mem_cat == 0])
        den <- s1 + s0
        p   <- if (den > 1e-9) s1 / den else 0.5
      }
      probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9, p))
      mem_obs  <- rbind(mem_obs, stim)
      mem_cat  <- c(mem_cat, cat_feedback[i])
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}

# ── Kalman simulator for alien task ──────────────────────────────────────────
# theta = (log_r) — 1 parameter
make_alien_kalman_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  function(theta) {
    r_val <- exp(theta[1])
    r_mat <- diag(r_val, n_features_alien)
    mu0   <- rep(0.5, n_features_alien); mu1 <- rep(0.5, n_features_alien)
    sig0  <- diag(1.0, n_features_alien); sig1 <- diag(1.0, n_features_alien)
    n     <- nrow(obs_matrix)
    probs <- numeric(n)
    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      x <- as.numeric(obs_matrix[i, ])
      lp0 <- tryCatch(mvtnorm::dmvnorm(x, mu0, sig0 + r_mat, log = TRUE),
                      error = function(e) -Inf)
      lp1 <- tryCatch(mvtnorm::dmvnorm(x, mu1, sig1 + r_mat, log = TRUE),
                      error = function(e) -Inf)
      if (all(is.finite(c(lp0, lp1)))) {
        lse <- log(exp(lp0) + exp(lp1))
        probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9, exp(lp1 - lse)))
      } else { probs[i] <- 0.5 }
      if (cat_feedback[i] == 1) {
        S <- sig1 + r_mat; K <- sig1 %*% solve(S)
        mu1  <- as.numeric(mu1 + K %*% (x - mu1))
        IK   <- diag(n_features_alien) - K
        sig1 <- (IK %*% sig1 %*% t(IK) + K %*% r_mat %*% t(K))
        sig1 <- (sig1 + t(sig1)) / 2
      } else {
        S <- sig0 + r_mat; K <- sig0 %*% solve(S)
        mu0  <- as.numeric(mu0 + K %*% (x - mu0))
        IK   <- diag(n_features_alien) - K
        sig0 <- (IK %*% sig0 %*% t(IK) + K %*% r_mat %*% t(K))
        sig0 <- (sig0 + t(sig0)) / 2
      }
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}

# ── Rule particle filter simulator for alien task ─────────────────────────────
# theta = (logit_eps) — 1 parameter (N_particles fixed at N_PARTICLES_FIXED)
make_alien_rule_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  n_feat <- ncol(obs_matrix)
  function(theta) {
    eps    <- plogis(theta[1])
    n_part <- N_PARTICLES_FIXED
    n      <- nrow(obs_matrix)

    gen_rule <- function() {
      nd <- sample(seq_len(min(MAX_RULE_DIMS_ALIEN, n_feat)), 1)
      dm <- sort(sample(seq_len(n_feat), nd, replace = FALSE))
      list(dims = dm, thresh = rep(0.5, nd),
           ops  = sample(c(">", "<"), nd, replace = TRUE),
           logic = if (nd > 1) sample(c("AND", "OR"), 1) else "AND",
           pred  = sample(0:1, 1))
    }
    eval_rule <- function(r, x) {
      tryCatch({
        ec <- function(d, t, o) switch(o, ">" = x[d] > t, "<" = x[d] < t, FALSE)
        if (length(r$dims) == 1) { cond <- ec(r$dims, r$thresh, r$ops)
        } else {
          cs <- mapply(ec, r$dims, r$thresh, r$ops)
          cond <- if (r$logic == "AND") all(cs) else any(cs)
        }
        ifelse(cond, r$pred, 1L - r$pred)
      }, error = function(e) sample(0:1, 1))
    }

    parts <- lapply(seq_len(n_part), function(i) gen_rule())
    wts   <- rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
    probs <- numeric(n)

    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      x  <- as.numeric(obs_matrix[i, ])
      pd <- vapply(parts, eval_rule, x = x, numeric(1))
      probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9,
        sum(wts * ifelse(pd == 1, 1 - eps, eps))))
      lk  <- vapply(parts, function(p) {
        pr <- eval_rule(p, x)
        if (pr == cat_feedback[i]) 1 - eps else eps
      }, numeric(1))
      nw  <- wts * lk; tot <- sum(nw)
      wts <- if (tot > 1e-9) nw / tot else rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
      ess <- 1 / sum(wts^2)
      if (!is.nan(ess) && ess < n_part * RESAMPLE_THRESHOLD) {
        idx   <- sample.int(n_part, n_part, replace = TRUE, prob = wts)
        parts <- parts[idx]; wts <- rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
      }
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}

train_alien_npe <- function(simulator_fn, prior_low, prior_high, n_params,
                             label, n_sims = n_train_alien) {
  cache_file <- sprintf("simdata/W17_npe_%s_%s_s%d.rds",
                        label, dec_example, sess_example)
  if (regenerate_fits || !file.exists(cache_file)) {
    cat("Generating", n_sims, "simulations for", label, "...\n")
    set.seed(42)
    thetas <- matrix(runif(n_sims * n_params, min = prior_low, max = prior_high),
                     ncol = n_params, byrow = TRUE)
    resps  <- future_map(seq_len(n_sims), function(i) simulator_fn(thetas[i, ]),
                         .options = furrr_options(seed = TRUE), .progress = TRUE)
    y_mat  <- do.call(rbind, resps)
    saveRDS(list(thetas = thetas, responses = y_mat), cache_file)
  } else {
    cached <- readRDS(cache_file); thetas <- cached$thetas; y_mat <- cached$responses
  }
  theta_t <- torch$tensor(thetas, dtype = torch$float32)
  x_t     <- torch$tensor(y_mat,  dtype = torch$float32)
  sbi_prior <- utils$BoxUniform(
    low  = torch$tensor(prior_low,  dtype = torch$float32),
    high = torch$tensor(prior_high, dtype = torch$float32)
  )
  inference_obj <- infer_sbi$SNPE(
    prior = sbi_prior,
    density_estimator = utils$posterior_nn(
      model = "nsf", hidden_features = 64L, num_transforms = 5L,
      embedding_net = torch$nn$Sequential(
        torch$nn$Linear(as.integer(n_trials_ex), 128L), torch$nn$ReLU(),
        torch$nn$Linear(128L, 64L), torch$nn$ReLU(),
        torch$nn$Linear(64L, 32L)
      )
    )
  )
  inference_obj$append_simulations(theta_t, x_t)
  dens <- inference_obj$train(training_batch_size = 256L, learning_rate = 5e-4,
                               show_train_summary = TRUE)
  list(posterior = inference_obj$build_posterior(dens),
       thetas = thetas, responses = y_mat)
}

## Phase 0: Structural Commitments for Human Data Analysis

> **Q1 — Is fitting the dangerous and nutritious decisions independently defensible with real human data?**
>
> Chapter 15's Phase 0 Q2 established the independent-fits commitment in the context of forward simulation: the agent learns the two dimensions with separate parameters and separate sequential states. With human data, this commitment has empirical consequences that Chapter 15 could not address.
>
> The commitment is *defensible* because: (a) feedback is provided for each dimension separately in the Tylén et al. (2023) task; (b) the two rules are logically independent (e.g., in Session 1, `f1 AND f3` for dangerous and `f4` for nutritious involve no shared features); (c) fitting independently is the simpler and more interpretable model.
>
> The commitment could fail if: (a) humans jointly allocate attention across both decisions in a way that creates cross-decision correlations in response patterns; (b) the stimulus features that matter for one decision are also attended to for the other (e.g., if `f4` is salient for nutritious, it may be sampled and influence the dangerous decision).
>
> **What we commit to here:** independent fits, with the understanding that the GCM's attention weight estimates may absorb any cross-dimension attention correlation as noise. A joint model could test this by checking whether attention weights estimated independently agree across the two decisions.

> **Q2 — What does "resetting model state across sessions" mean cognitively, and is it justified?**
>
> The Kalman filter's prototype state is reset between sessions (new priors `mu = 0.5`, `sigma = I`). The GCM's exemplar memory is also reset — the model starts Session 2 with no memory of Session 1 exemplars. This is a strong cognitive assumption: in reality, participants carry some representation from one session to the next (the stimuli are the same aliens; only the rules change).
>
> We accept the reset assumption for two reasons: (a) the category rules change completely between sessions (the same alien that was dangerous in Session 1 may be peaceful in Session 2), so exemplar memories from Session 1 are actively misleading in Session 2; (b) the fitting is per-session, which means each session's model is calibrated to that session's rule structure independently, allowing us to compare model fits across sessions without confounding.
>
> **What we commit to here:** per-session independent fits with state resets. The models do not carry memory across sessions. This is a design choice with cognitive content: we are testing whether each model can explain within-session learning, not cross-session transfer.

> **Q3 — Why use multilevel GCM but single-subject Kalman and rule models?**
>
> The multilevel GCM from Chapter 12 pools information across participants through a hierarchical prior on attention weights and sensitivity. The Kalman and rule models are fitted per-subject with no pooling. This creates a structural asymmetry:
>
> * **For the GCM**: partial pooling regularizes individual estimates, reducing overfitting and improving out-of-sample prediction especially for participants with few trials. The multilevel structure also produces a population-level summary of attention weights that is directly interpretable.
> * **For the Kalman and rule models**: per-subject fits are unregularized. For participants with short or homogeneous trial sequences, the posteriors may be broad and uninformative.
>
> This asymmetry **biases the LOO comparison in favor of the GCM** by giving it an effective parameter reduction that the other models do not have. The honest interpretation is: the GCM with multilevel pooling competes against the Kalman and rule models without pooling. A fully fair comparison would require multilevel versions of all three models, which we do not develop here.
>
> **What we commit to here:** we use the available multilevel GCM and per-subject Kalman/rule models, acknowledge the asymmetry explicitly in the model comparison section, and note that any ELPD advantage for the GCM should be interpreted with this caveat.

> **Q4 — What do we know about the AlienData.txt provenance and expected format?**
>
> The data file is from Tylén et al. (2023). Key known properties:
>
> * One row per trial; columns include `subject`, `condition`, `session`, `cycle` (block number), `test` (0 = training with feedback, 1 = test without feedback), `stimulus` (filename containing the 5-digit binary feature string), `response` (1–4, encoding all combinations of dangerous/nutritious), `category` (irrelevant here), `dangerous` and `nutricious` (true labels, note the spelling).
> * `condition == 1` = individual participants; `condition == 2` = pairs.
> * `test == 0` trials are used for all model fitting (training trials only).
> * The `nutricious` spelling is present in the raw file; we rename it to `nutritious` in cleaning.
> * The rule verification block below is the halt-on-fail guard: if the assumed rules do not match the data labels, all downstream fitting is invalid and the chapter stops.
>
> **What we commit to here:** we load the data, verify the stimulus parsing, verify the rule labelling, and halt if any verification fails. These checks mirror the Phase 0 Q3 verification in Chapter 15.

**Summary of Phase 0 commitments.** Independent fits per decision dimension and per session, with state resets between sessions. Multilevel GCM vs. per-subject Kalman/rule creates a pooling asymmetry that favors the GCM in LOO comparison. Prior hyperparameters match Chapters 14 and 16 (`LOGIT_ERROR_PRIOR_MEAN = 0.0`, `SD = 1.5`). Data verified against rules before any fitting proceeds.

## 4. Fitting Models to Human Data (Track 1)

We fit all three Stan models to the real human data. This is the fulfilment of the pipeline established in Chapters 12–16: prior predictive check → SBC → parameter recovery → single-agent fit → multilevel fit → LOO comparison, all now applied to empirical data.

### Design Choices

**Two-decision structure**: We fit each model **separately for each decision dimension** within each session (Phase 0 Q1).

**Sequential structure within sessions**: Each session resets the model state (Phase 0 Q2).

**Multilevel vs. single-subject**: The multilevel GCM (Ch. 13, `ch12_gcm_ml.stan`) is used for all participants jointly. Kalman and rule models are fitted per-subject (Phase 0 Q3 asymmetry).

```{r ch17_prep_gcm_data}
build_gcm_ml_data <- function(df, decision_col) {
  resp_col <- paste0("inferred_", decision_col)

  df_sorted <- df |>
    mutate(subj_id_stan = as.integer(factor(subject))) |>
```

> **Note on the Stan rule model with 5 features**: The Stan rule model in
> Chapter 14 hardcodes a candidate rule set over a 2D feature space. For the
> 5D Alien task, the candidate rule set must be expanded to include the
> session-specific relevant rules. A general approach enumerates all
> single-feature and two-feature threshold rules over the five binary
> dimensions: $5 \times 2 + \binom{5}{2} \times 2 \times 2 = 10 + 40 = 50$
> candidate rules. For binary features all thresholds are 0.5, so each rule
> reduces to testing whether one or two features equal 0 or 1. We leave this
> extension as an exercise; the rule model here uses the session-specific
> candidate rules derived from the rule-check above.

```{r ch17_fit_models}
run_fit_single <- function(mod, data, seed, adapt_delta = 0.9,
                           iter_w = 800, iter_s = 1000) {
  pf <- tryCatch(
    mod$pathfinder(data = data, seed = seed, num_paths = 4, refresh = 0),
    error = function(e) NULL
  )
  mod$sample(
    data            = data,
    init            = if (!is.null(pf)) pf else 0.5,
    seed            = seed,
    chains          = 2,
    parallel_chains = 2,
    iter_warmup     = iter_w,
    iter_sampling   = iter_s,
    refresh         = 0,
    adapt_delta     = adapt_delta
  )
}
```

### LFO-CV for Path-Dependent Models

The discrepancy between LOO and LFO-CV measures the magnitude of the path-dependence bias.

> equivalent on this dataset. For path-dependent models, standard LOO ELPD is
> a lower bound on the true LFO-CV ELPD (leaving out a future block is a harder

### Forward Simulators (Alien Task)

```{r ch17_alien_simulators, eval=run_npe_track}
# Simulators re-defined from Ch. 15 for self-containment.
# In a shared-code workflow these would be sourced from a common file.

n_features_alien <- 5L

# ── GCM simulator for alien task ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# theta = (logit_w1, ..., logit_w4, log_c) — 5 params (w5 = 1 - sum(w1:w4))
make_alien_gcm_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  function(theta) {
    # Softmax over 5 weights via log-sum-exp normalisation
    raw_w <- c(theta[1:4], 0)  # w5 is reference; use softmax
    w     <- exp(raw_w - log(sum(exp(raw_w))))
    c_val <- exp(theta[5])
    n <- nrow(obs_matrix)
    probs <- numeric(n)
    mem_obs <- matrix(NA_real_, 0, n_features_alien)
    mem_cat <- numeric(0)
    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      stim  <- obs_matrix[i, ]
      n_mem <- nrow(mem_obs)
      if (n_mem == 0 || length(unique(mem_cat)) < 2) {
        p <- 0.5
      } else {
        sims <- vapply(seq_len(n_mem), function(e)
          exp(-c_val * sum(w * abs(stim - mem_obs[e, ]))), numeric(1))
        s1 <- sum(sims[mem_cat == 1]); s0 <- sum(sims[mem_cat == 0])
        den <- s1 + s0
        p   <- if (den > 1e-9) s1 / den else 0.5
      }
      probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9, p))
      mem_obs  <- rbind(mem_obs, stim)
      mem_cat  <- c(mem_cat, cat_feedback[i])
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}

# ── Kalman simulator for alien task ──────────────────────────────────────────
# theta = (log_r) — 1 parameter
make_alien_kalman_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  function(theta) {
    r_val <- exp(theta[1])
    r_mat <- diag(r_val, n_features_alien)
    mu0   <- rep(0.5, n_features_alien); mu1 <- rep(0.5, n_features_alien)
    sig0  <- diag(1.0, n_features_alien); sig1 <- diag(1.0, n_features_alien)
    n     <- nrow(obs_matrix)
    probs <- numeric(n)
    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      x <- as.numeric(obs_matrix[i, ])
      lp0 <- tryCatch(mvtnorm::dmvnorm(x, mu0, sig0 + r_mat, log = TRUE),
                      error = function(e) -Inf)
      lp1 <- tryCatch(mvtnorm::dmvnorm(x, mu1, sig1 + r_mat, log = TRUE),
                      error = function(e) -Inf)
      if (all(is.finite(c(lp0, lp1)))) {
        lse <- log(exp(lp0) + exp(lp1))
        probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9, exp(lp1 - lse)))
      } else { probs[i] <- 0.5 }
      if (cat_feedback[i] == 1) {
        S <- sig1 + r_mat; K <- sig1 %*% solve(S)
        mu1  <- as.numeric(mu1 + K %*% (x - mu1))
        IK   <- diag(n_features_alien) - K
        sig1 <- (IK %*% sig1 %*% t(IK) + K %*% r_mat %*% t(K))
        sig1 <- (sig1 + t(sig1)) / 2
      } else {
        S <- sig0 + r_mat; K <- sig0 %*% solve(S)
        mu0  <- as.numeric(mu0 + K %*% (x - mu0))
        IK   <- diag(n_features_alien) - K
        sig0 <- (IK %*% sig0 %*% t(IK) + K %*% r_mat %*% t(K))
        sig0 <- (sig0 + t(sig0)) / 2
      }
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}

# ── Rule particle filter simulator for alien task ─────────────────────────────
# theta = (logit_eps) — 1 parameter (N_particles fixed at N_PARTICLES_FIXED)
make_alien_rule_simulator <- function(obs_matrix, cat_feedback) {
  n_feat <- ncol(obs_matrix)
  function(theta) {
    eps    <- plogis(theta[1])
    n_part <- N_PARTICLES_FIXED
    n      <- nrow(obs_matrix)

    gen_rule <- function() {
      nd <- sample(seq_len(min(MAX_RULE_DIMS_ALIEN, n_feat)), 1)
      dm <- sort(sample(seq_len(n_feat), nd, replace = FALSE))
      list(dims = dm, thresh = rep(0.5, nd),
           ops  = sample(c(">", "<"), nd, replace = TRUE),
           logic = if (nd > 1) sample(c("AND", "OR"), 1) else "AND",
           pred  = sample(0:1, 1))
    }
    eval_rule <- function(r, x) {
      tryCatch({
        ec <- function(d, t, o) switch(o, ">" = x[d] > t, "<" = x[d] < t, FALSE)
        if (length(r$dims) == 1) { cond <- ec(r$dims, r$thresh, r$ops)
        } else {
          cs <- mapply(ec, r$dims, r$thresh, r$ops)
          cond <- if (r$logic == "AND") all(cs) else any(cs)
        }
        ifelse(cond, r$pred, 1L - r$pred)
      }, error = function(e) sample(0:1, 1))
    }

    parts <- lapply(seq_len(n_part), function(i) gen_rule())
    wts   <- rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
    probs <- numeric(n)

    for (i in seq_len(n)) {
      x  <- as.numeric(obs_matrix[i, ])
      pd <- vapply(parts, eval_rule, x = x, numeric(1))
      probs[i] <- pmax(1e-9, pmin(1 - 1e-9,
        sum(wts * ifelse(pd == 1, 1 - eps, eps))))
      lk  <- vapply(parts, function(p) {
        pr <- eval_rule(p, x)
        if (pr == cat_feedback[i]) 1 - eps else eps
      }, numeric(1))
      nw  <- wts * lk; tot <- sum(nw)
      wts <- if (tot > 1e-9) nw / tot else rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
      ess <- 1 / sum(wts^2)
      if (!is.nan(ess) && ess < n_part * RESAMPLE_THRESHOLD) {
        idx   <- sample.int(n_part, n_part, replace = TRUE, prob = wts)
        parts <- parts[idx]; wts <- rep(1 / n_part, n_part)
      }
    }
    rbinom(n, 1, probs)
  }
}
```

```{r ch17_npe_train_human, eval=run_npe_track}
train_alien_npe <- function(simulator_fn, prior_low, prior_high, n_params,
                             label, n_sims = n_train_alien) {
  cache_file <- sprintf("simdata/W17_npe_%s_%s_s%d.rds",
                        label, dec_example, sess_example)
  if (regenerate_fits || !file.exists(cache_file)) {
    cat("Generating", n_sims, "simulations for", label, "...\n")
    set.seed(42)
    thetas <- matrix(runif(n_sims * n_params, min = prior_low, max = prior_high),
                     ncol = n_params, byrow = TRUE)
    resps  <- future_map(seq_len(n_sims), function(i) simulator_fn(thetas[i, ]),
                         .options = furrr_options(seed = TRUE), .progress = TRUE)
    y_mat  <- do.call(rbind, resps)
    saveRDS(list(thetas = thetas, responses = y_mat), cache_file)
  } else {
    cached <- readRDS(cache_file); thetas <- cached$thetas; y_mat <- cached$responses
  }
  theta_t <- torch$tensor(thetas, dtype = torch$float32)
  x_t     <- torch$tensor(y_mat,  dtype = torch$float32)
  sbi_prior <- utils$BoxUniform(
    low  = torch$tensor(prior_low,  dtype = torch$float32),
    high = torch$tensor(prior_high, dtype = torch$float32)
  )
  inference_obj <- infer_sbi$SNPE(
    prior = sbi_prior,
    density_estimator = utils$posterior_nn(
      model = "nsf", hidden_features = 64L, num_transforms = 5L,
      embedding_net = torch$nn$Sequential(
        torch$nn$Linear(as.integer(n_trials_ex), 128L), torch$nn$ReLU(),
        torch$nn$Linear(128L, 64L), torch$nn$ReLU(),
        torch$nn$Linear(64L, 32L)
      )
    )
  )
  inference_obj$append_simulations(theta_t, x_t)
  dens <- inference_obj$train(training_batch_size = 256L, learning_rate = 5e-4,
                               show_train_summary = TRUE)
  list(posterior = inference_obj$build_posterior(dens),
       thetas = thetas, responses = y_mat)
}
```

## 9. Synthesis and Discussion

### Which Model Best Accounts for Human Categorization?

The stacking weights from Section 5 provide a Track 1 summary: models with near-zero stacking weight add no predictive information beyond the other models for this session and decision. The Track 2 NPE posteriors (when `run_npe_track = TRUE`) provide the cognitive-theory complement: which model's forward simulator best accounts for individual response patterns.

**Key predictions from Chapter 15** to evaluate:

**GCM predictions**: Optimal attention weights should concentrate on the truly diagnostic features. Evaluate with the posterior attention weight plots in Section 6.

**Kalman filter predictions**: Model should struggle most with Session 2 nutritious and Session 3 nutritious rules where the category distribution is not unimodal.

**Rule model predictions**: Performance should be highest in Session 1 where rules are simple conjunctions. The model may show abrupt learning patterns.

### Individual Differences

The individual-level fits tell a richer story than the population average. Participants may genuinely differ in strategy: some showing GCM-like attention focusing (high estimated $c$, weights concentrated on diagnostic features), others showing Kalman-like smooth prototype updating (low $r$), and others showing rule-like learning (low $\varepsilon$, abrupt performance changes).

### The Condition Effect (Individual vs. Pair)

Do pairs show different model fit patterns from individuals? Possible hypotheses:

* **Cognitive division of labour**: Pairs may divide attention between the dangerous and nutritious dimensions.
* **Rule verbalization**: Pairs may more easily articulate and apply explicit rules, leading to higher stacking weight for the rule model.
* **Error correction**: Pairs may catch each other's errors, leading to lower estimated $\varepsilon$ or lower $r$.
